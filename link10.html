Theoretical Framework: The Time-Dependent Schr√∂dinger Equation (TDSE)

The fundamental equation governing the **simulation** is the TDSE. Since the Kinetic ($\hat{T}$) and Potential ($\hat{V}$) operators do not commute, we rely on the high-performance numerical technique known as the **Split-Step Fourier Method (SSFM)**.

1. The TDSE and Hamiltonian Operator

The Hamiltonian operator ($\hat{H}$) represents the total energy, and its action on the wave function ($\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)$) dictates the time evolution being modeled in the simulation.

$$ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) = \hat{H} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) $$ $$ \hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}, t) $$

Where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, $m$ is the particle mass, $\nabla^2$ is the Laplacian, and $V$ is the potential energy.

2. The Split-Step Fourier Method (SSFM) for Simulation

The SSFM is the workhorse of this **simulation**. It cleverly splits the total time evolution $\hat{U}(\Delta t)$ into two commutative steps ($\hat{U}_V$ and $\hat{U}_T$), allowing the non-commuting operators to be handled sequentially for computational stability and speed. The formal solution for a small time step $\Delta t$ is approximated by:

$$ \Psi(t+\Delta t) \approx e^{-i \hat{V} \Delta t / \hbar} e^{-i \hat{T} \Delta t / \hbar} \Psi(t) $$

Step 1: Kinetic Energy Propagation (Momentum Space)

The kinetic operator ($\hat{T}$) is applied efficiently in **momentum space** ($\mathbf{p}$), where it is a simple multiplication. This requires a Fourier Transform ($\mathcal{F}$) integral, which is implemented using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).

$$ \tilde{\Psi}(\mathbf{p}, t) = \mathcal{F} \left\{ \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) \right\} = \frac{1}{(2\pi \hbar)^{3/2}} \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} \Psi(\mathbf{r}, t) e^{-i \mathbf{p} \cdot \mathbf{r} / \hbar} \, d\mathbf{r} $$ $$ \text{Evolution:} \quad \tilde{\Psi}_{\text{final}} = e^{-i \left( \frac{p^2}{2m} \right) \Delta t / \hbar} \tilde{\Psi}(\mathbf{p}, t) $$

Step 2: Potential Energy Propagation (Position Space)

The potential operator ($\hat{V}$) is applied in **position space** ($\mathbf{r}$) where it acts as a phase change multiplier on the wave function.

$$ \Psi_{\text{final}}(\mathbf{r}, t+\Delta t) = e^{-i V(\mathbf{r}, t) \Delta t / \hbar} \Psi_{\text{intermediate}}(\mathbf{r}, t) $$

3. Probability Density and Interference

The observable quantity visualized by the **simulation** is the **probability density** $\rho(\mathbf{r}, t)$, which shows where the particle is likely to be found. The striking patterns seen are a direct result of the complex phases of the wave function interfering with itself.

$$ \rho(\mathbf{r}, t) = |\Psi(\mathbf{r}, t)|^2 = \Psi^* \Psi $$

The ability to simulate complex potential landscapes is key to studying modern quantum systems.
